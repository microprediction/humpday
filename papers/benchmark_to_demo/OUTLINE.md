# Do benchmark rankings predict demonstration rankings?

A short empirical paper, companion to `papers/dfo_recommender/`, that
asks the question the first paper deferred: **when a derivative-free
optimizer wins on the standard benchmark suite, does it win on
realistic engineering / physics demonstration tasks?**

The benchmark suite is HumpDay's 12-objective grid (sphere, Rosenbrock,
..., rotated_ackley). The demonstration tasks are the 17 interactive
demos in `docs/applications/`. This paper measures correlation between
the two rankings on the subset of demos we can port to Python.

All analysis regenerated by
`python papers/benchmark_to_demo/analysis.py`. Numbers in this
outline are current as of the demo set checked in at
`papers/benchmark_to_demo/demos.py`.

## Headline finding

Across **seven demos** ported so far, the **pooled Spearman ρ between
benchmark Borda and demonstration Borda is 0.316** — weak positive
correlation overall. Per demo the picture is much messier:

| Demo | n_dim | Spearman ρ | Demo winner | Grid recommendation | Match? |
|--|--|--|--|--|--|
| welded_beam | 4 | 0.006 | ParticleSwarm | Powell | — |
| robot_arm | 6 | **−0.418** | FireflyAlgorithm | Powell | — |
| slingshot | 2 | 0.769 | PRIMA_UOBYQA | PRIMA_NEWUOA | — |
| brachistochrone | 10 | 0.635 | LBFGSB | PRIMA_NEWUOA | — |
| battery_dispatch | 24 | −0.036 | Rechenberg | PRIMA_BOBYQA | — |
| **reactor_tprofile** | 10 | **0.919** | PRIMA_NEWUOA | PRIMA_NEWUOA | **✓** |
| wind_farm | 16 | −0.139 | PatternSearch | PRIMA_NEWUOA | — |

**The grid matches the actual demo winner on 1 of 7 demos.** The one
match (`reactor_tprofile`) is also the only demo with extremely high
correlation (ρ = 0.919) — a smooth, well-conditioned chemistry
kinetics problem with a monotonic temperature-to-yield relationship.
Exactly what the benchmark suite is built for.

Everything with **constraint penalties, dynamics, or geometric
structure** shows weak or negative correlation:

- **robot_arm** anti-correlation (ρ = −0.418): benchmark-favoured
  PRIMA / Powell got the worst results (-58 to -88 out of 100), while
  benchmark-mid-tier Firefly (-96) and BayesianOpt (-91) won. The
  collision penalty + reach-payoff landscape kills smooth-function
  trust-region methods.
- **battery_dispatch** zero correlation (ρ = −0.036): a 24-D *smooth*
  scheduling problem with state-of-charge bounds. The grid has no
  comparable structure — its benchmarks are mostly low-dim
  unconstrained.
- **wind_farm** small negative (ρ = −0.139): the spacing penalty
  creates the same discontinuity that hurt robot_arm. PRIMA_NEWUOA
  catastrophically fails here (returns +50.25 while every other
  algorithm reaches -90 to -98).

The **clean takeaway**: benchmark rankings predict performance on
real tasks only when those tasks happen to be smooth, monotonic, and
unconstrained — i.e., already representable by the benchmark suite.
On any task with structure the benchmarks don't capture (dynamics,
penalties, packing, scheduling), the recommender's pick is at best
uncorrelated and at worst actively misleading.

## Structure

### § 1 Introduction

The default-algorithm-recommendation problem (companion paper
`papers/dfo_recommender/`) treats benchmark ranking as ground truth.
This paper asks whether that's defensible. We adopt the same
22-algorithm catalogue, the same Borda mean-rank scoring, and the
same grid cells as `papers/dfo_recommender/` — only the objective set
changes.

The hypothesis is **negative**: benchmark rankings do not transfer
to engineering / physics tasks, even ones that match the benchmark
suite's `(n_dim, n_trials)` cell exactly.

### § 2 Method

Port each demo's `objective(u): [0,1]^n → R` from
`docs/applications/<demo>.html` to Python (in
`papers/benchmark_to_demo/demos.py`). Validate against the demo's
published optimum or known-feasible point. Run every eligible
HumpDay algorithm at the demo's suggested `n_trials` for `N_SEEDS`
seeds. Compute per-demo Borda rank for each algorithm. Compare to
the recommendation grid's Borda at the corresponding cell via
Spearman ρ.

Demos ported so far (seven; more to follow):

| Demo | n_dim | n_trials | Real-world correspondence |
|--|--|--|--|
| welded_beam | 4 | 200 | Classical 4-D structural optimization benchmark (Coello 2000) |
| robot_arm | 6 | 200 | 6-DOF inverse kinematics with obstacle avoidance |
| slingshot | 2 | 50 | Projectile aiming under gravity |
| brachistochrone | 10 | 200 | Discretised curve-of-fastest-descent |
| battery_dispatch | 24 | 200 | 24-hour battery charge/discharge schedule, NYISO-Zone-J prices |
| reactor_tprofile | 10 | 200 | A→B→C series reactor temperature profile, Arrhenius kinetics |
| wind_farm | 16 | 200 | 8-turbine placement, Jensen wake model + 12-bin wind rose |

### § 3 Experiments

#### § 3.1 Per-demo Spearman correlation (Table 1)

Re-stated from the headline. The two demos where the benchmark
ranking is informative (slingshot ρ = 0.77, brachistochrone ρ = 0.64)
are both well-conditioned, smooth optimization problems closely
resembling the benchmark suite's standard objectives. The two where
it fails (welded_beam ρ ≈ 0, robot_arm ρ < 0) have **constraint
penalties** or **discrete features** (collisions, feasibility
boundaries) that change the optimization landscape's character.

#### § 3.2 Per-demo winners are never the recommended algorithm (Table 2)

The grid's recommendation matches the demo winner on **0 of 4
demos** in this dataset. Even where the correlation is positive,
the recommender's _argmin_ rarely matches the demo's _argmin_.
This is a stronger result than the pooled correlation: even when
the rankings broadly agree, the top picks diverge.

#### § 3.3 Failure-mode breakdown

The robot_arm anti-correlation deserves its own treatment. The
"-0" cells (NelderMead, LBFGSB, HillClimbing, Rechenberg,
CoordinateDescent, PatternSearch) are all local-search algorithms
that started in an infeasible pose (collisions everywhere) and
never improved. The benchmark suite contains no benchmark with
this property — every standard benchmark has a smooth interior
the local algorithms can crawl. **The benchmark suite under-
represents non-smooth penalty landscapes**, and recommendations
trained on it inherit the bias.

### § 4 Discussion

- **The benchmark recommendation matters most where it matters least:**
  benchmark/demo correlation is strongest on the simplest demos
  (slingshot 2-D, brachistochrone 10-D smooth), where any reasonable
  algorithm wins.
- **The recommender is fragile on realistic tasks:** for the demos
  with non-trivial structure (welded_beam, robot_arm), benchmark
  Borda is essentially useless or actively misleading.
- **Implications for the cost-aware recommender** (`papers/dfo_recommender/`
  § 4): the soft cost-weighted Borda variant doesn't help here because
  the failure isn't in the cost-vs-quality trade-off — it's in the
  *quality* signal itself.
- **Methodological recommendation:** a real recommender should
  combine benchmark Borda with a small number of **classifier
  features** of the user's objective (separable vs. non-separable,
  smooth vs. discontinuous penalty, constrained vs. unconstrained,
  …). Inferring these from the timing call's behavior, or from a
  few diagnostic probes, is the natural follow-up.

### § 5 Limitations

- 4 demos is a small sample. Five-ten more should be tractable.
- The Python ports may diverge subtly from the JS versions; we
  validated only welded_beam against its published optimum.
- No statistical correction for multiple comparisons across demos
  (the per-demo p-values are nominal, not family-wise).
- Each demo is run with `N_SEEDS = 3`; bumping to 10 would tighten
  the Borda ranks but not change the qualitative picture.

## What's next

- (a) Port 4–6 more demos (bowling, curling, pool, slingshot already
  done; ones still to port: trebuchet, free-kick, mini-golf,
  rocket-landing, bridge-truss, wind-farm). Particularly
  bowling/curling/pool because they have rich physics simulators
  the benchmark suite can't approximate.
- (b) Per-algorithm "demo gap" report — which algorithms gain or
  lose the most going from benchmarks to demos?
- (c) Try the cost-aware recommender from
  `papers/dfo_recommender/` § 4 on the demos. Does it help, hurt,
  or no-op?
- (d) Diagnostic-probe-based recommendation: instead of just
  timing the objective once, run a few small probes (e.g. randomly
  sample 5 points, check whether the gradient is informative)
  and use those features alongside the grid.
