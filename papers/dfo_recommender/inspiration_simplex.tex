\begin{abstract}
\noindent A note that promises nothing. We describe a small construction --- a
simplex whose vertices are established algorithms and whose interior is every
blend of them an LLM can express --- and then, because the construction is a
recipe for combining inspirations, we apply it to itself.
\end{abstract}

\section{The idea (such as it is)}

Put a few \emph{named, established algorithms} at the vertices of a simplex ---
say Nelder--Mead, Differential Evolution, CMA-ES. A point inside the simplex is a
set of barycentric coordinates: a \emph{recipe} that says ``60\% this, 30\% that,
10\% the other.'' Hand those coordinates to a language model and ask it not to
average parameters but to \textbf{write a new optimizer that blends the ideas} ---
the search discipline of one, the population logic of another, the step control of
a third. The vertices are pure inspirations; the interior is everything you can
say by mixing them. (The working prototype is \texttt{simplex\_blend.py}; the
host-and-graft asymmetric prompt is the only part that took real thought.)

The one mildly interesting empirical question --- still open, still unpromised ---
is whether the \textbf{interior is richer than the vertices}: does some blend beat
every pure ancestor, or is the inside of the simplex just a soggy compromise? We
have a regret surface (\texttt{runs/simplex\_overnight.json}) and no firm
conclusion, which is exactly where an honest note should leave it.

\section{The part that made me laugh}

The method is a recipe for combining sources of inspiration. So the method should
be applied to \emph{itself}. The idea of the Inspiration Simplex obviously needs
to \textbf{evolve inside an Inspiration Simplex} --- one whose vertices are not
optimizers but \textbf{established brainstorming techniques}. Candidate vertices:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{SCAMPER} --- Substitute / Combine / Adapt / Modify /
    Put-to-other-use / Eliminate / Reverse. The workmanlike vertex.
  \item \textbf{TRIZ} --- resolve the contradiction; consult the inventive
    principles. The vertex that insists there is a \emph{right} answer hiding in a
    table.
  \item \textbf{Six Thinking Hats} --- rotate deliberately through optimism,
    caution, data, feeling, process. The vertex that schedules its own moods.
  \item \textbf{Synectics} --- make the strange familiar and the familiar strange;
    reason by analogy. The vertex most likely to mention octopuses.
  \item \textbf{Oblique Strategies} --- draw a card, obey it. (``Honour thy error
    as a hidden intention.'') The random-restart vertex.
  \item \textbf{Morphological analysis} (the Zwicky box) --- enumerate the
    dimensions, take the cross-product, stare. Pleasingly, \emph{itself} a
    combinatorial blending method, which makes putting it on the simplex either
    elegant or cheating.
\end{itemize}

A barycentric point is then a brainstorming \emph{style}: ``40\% TRIZ-rigour, 30\%
Oblique-provocation, 30\% Synectics-analogy,'' handed to a model that must run that
blended style on the question \emph{``how should the Inspiration Simplex be
improved?''}

\section{The infinite regress, acknowledged and declined}

Yes: the meta-simplex could itself be improved by a meta-meta-simplex whose
vertices are \emph{methods for improving brainstorming methods}, and so on up the
tower. We note the tower exists. We decline to climb it. One level of
self-reference is whimsy; two is a research-group in-joke; three is a stack
overflow.

\section{What this note promises}

Nothing. No benchmark, no win, no claim that mixing brainstorming techniques
produces better ideas than picking a good one and getting on with it. It is
plausible the best point in the brainstorming simplex is a vertex --- that you
should just \emph{do TRIZ properly} --- and that the interior, here as with the
optimizers, is mostly soggy compromise. That would also be a fine result.

The note exists only so that, if the interior ever does turn out to be richer than
the vertices --- for optimizers, for ideas, or for ideas about ideas --- we wrote
the joke down first.
