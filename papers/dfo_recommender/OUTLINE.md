# Default algorithm selection for derivative-free black-box optimization

A short empirical paper evaluating `humpday.eligibility.recommend()` — the
auto-selection logic shipped in HumpDay v0.20.0 / v0.21.0 — on a
12-objective benchmark suite, then asking whether those rankings
transfer to seven physics-flavoured "demonstration" tasks ported from
the HumpDay demo gallery.

All numbers are regenerated by
`python papers/dfo_recommender/analysis.py` from
`benchmarks/recommendation_grid.json` and
`papers/dfo_recommender/demo_results.json`.

## Headline claims

1. **In-distribution, the recommender works.** It matches the naive
   (solution-quality) oracle on **68 %** of (cell × eval-time) instances
   with **median Borda regret 0**. The pre-v0.20 rule-based heuristic
   (`suggest_pure`) matches **0 %** and is 6.85× worse. (§ 3)
2. **The recommender is not overfit to its objective suite.** Leave-one-
   objective-out cross-validation reproduces the same median solution-
   quality ratio (≈ 1.0). (§ 3.2)
3. **Wall-clock honesty matters most at sub-100 µs.** The naive vs cost-
   aware oracles disagree on 55 % of cells at 100 µs. A soft cost-
   weighted Borda variant closes most of that gap, and a per-eval-time
   λ schedule beats every fixed λ (72.6 % vs 70.1 % cost-aware match).
   The schedule ships as `cost_weight="auto"` in
   `humpday.eligibility.recommend`. (§ 4)
4. **Out-of-distribution, the recommender is barely better than chance.**
   On eight real-task demos (welded beam, 6-D robot-arm reach,
   slingshot, brachistochrone, 24-h battery dispatch, reactor T-profile,
   16-D wind-farm layout, and a 17-dim MLP trained to fit XOR), pooled
   Spearman ρ between demo Borda and grid Borda is **0.301** (n = 163).
   The recommender's top pick matches the demo winner on **1 of 8**. (§ 5)
5. **One failure is structural, not stochastic.** PRIMA_NEWUOA — the
   recommender's pick for wind_farm — returns identical objective
   50.249 across all three seeds. Forensics show 7/8 turbines crammed
   into a single horizontal band and 15/28 pairwise spacing violations:
   the deterministic trust-region trajectory of NEWUOA collides with a
   highly multimodal, heavily constraint-penalised geometric landscape.
   This is a class finding for quadratic-model methods on coupled
   geometric placement. (§ 6)
6. **A tiny landscape probe carries enough signal to slightly beat the
   dimension-only recommender.** Four scale-free features extracted
   from k = 5 random samples + 4 targeted perturbations (9 extra evals,
   4.5 % overhead on a 200-trial budget) feed a LOO 2-NN top-3-vote
   classifier. On the 8-demo OOD set it scores mean rank 7.00 vs the
   grid's 7.12, top-5 5/8 vs 4/8. The gain is small because 8 demos
   is too few for the classifier; the LOO pairings cluster sensibly
   (mlp_xor↔wind_farm, slingshot↔reactor, welded_beam↔robot_arm) so
   the signal exists. (§ 5.3)

## Structure

### § 1 Introduction

The default-algorithm problem: a user calls a black-box optimizer
without knowing the structure of their objective. Most libraries pick a
fixed algorithm:

- `scipy.optimize.minimize`: Nelder-Mead (no derivative-free dim awareness)
- `scikit-optimize.gp_minimize`: Bayesian optimization (cubic in n_obs)
- `nevergrad.optimizers.NGOpt`: a portfolio that switches on problem size

HumpDay v0.21.0 introduces a precomputed recommendation grid: for each
`(n_dim, n_trials)` cell, every algorithm's Borda mean-rank across a
12-objective benchmark suite is stored; the recommender filters by
dimensional cap and overhead tier, then picks the Borda winner. This
paper asks two questions, in order: (i) does that design work *on the
distribution it was trained on*, and (ii) does it transfer to anything
else.

### § 2 Method

- **Algorithms**: 22 (`PURE_OPTIMIZERS`).
- **Benchmark objectives**: 12 unit-cube benchmarks (sphere, Rosenbrock,
  Rastrigin, Griewank, Salomon, Ackley, Schwefel, Michalewicz,
  Styblinski-Tang, plus three rotated variants).
- **Grid**: 11 dimensions × 3 trial budgets = 33 cells; each entry is
  median best-value + Borda mean-rank across 3 seeds and 12 objectives.
- **Filters**: dimensional cap, minimum trials, overhead tier
  (eval-time threshold).
- **Recommender**: among eligible algorithms, pick the one with the
  lowest Borda score on the nearest cell.
- **Demonstration tasks** (§ 5): eight pure-stdlib objectives in
  `papers/dfo_recommender/demos.py` — welded beam (4D), robot arm reach
  (6D), slingshot (2D), brachistochrone (10D), 24-h battery dispatch
  (24D), reactor T-profile (10D), 16-D wind-farm layout, and a 17-dim
  MLP-on-XOR (2-4-1 tanh/sigmoid net, MSE loss over the four XOR
  examples; no torch/numpy dependency). Welded beam validated against
  the Coello/Liang published optimum (1.7249 vs 1.725).

### § 3 In-distribution: the recommender on its training distribution

#### § 3.1 Oracle gap

Recommender vs naive Borda oracle vs three baselines on 198 instances.

| Strategy | Match | Median Borda regret | Median solution ratio |
|--|--|--|--|
| recommender | 68 % | 0.00 | 1.00 |
| fixed_best (PRIMA_BOBYQA) | 28 % | 1.28 | 1.37 |
| suggest_pure (pre-v0.20 rule) | 0 % | 6.54 | 6.85 |
| random | 5 % | 9.97 | 8.77 |

Even "always pick PRIMA_BOBYQA" — the single best algorithm by average
Borda — trails the recommender substantially. The rule-based heuristic
that the recommender replaced was strikingly bad: it never matched the
oracle and was 6.85× worse on median solution quality.

#### § 3.2 Leave-one-objective-out cross-validation

Hold each of the 12 benchmark objectives out, rebuild Borda from the
remaining 11, and re-pick. Median solution-quality ratio against the
per-objective oracle ≈ 1.0 across 9 of 12 objectives — the Borda
recommender is not overfit to its objective suite. Mean ratios are
heavy-tailed in a few cases; those are diagnosed in § 4 once cost-aware
scoring is in scope.

### § 4 Wall-clock honesty

#### § 4.1 The 100 µs gap

At sub-100 µs eval times the recommender stops following the naive
oracle because tier-2+ algorithms would consume more wall-clock than
the user's objective. Against a wall-clock-aware oracle (algorithm's
own overhead within 1× user wall-clock):

| eval_time | Match vs naive | Match vs cost-aware | Oracles disagree |
|--|--|--|--|
| 10 µs | 12 % | 72 % | 88 % |
| 100 µs | 88 % | 39 % | 55 % |
| 1 ms | 97 % | 70 % | 27 % |
| 10 ms | 97 % | 91 % | 6 % |
| 1 s | 97 % | 97 % | 0 % |

At 10 µs the recommender is doing the right thing (72 % cost-aware
match) even though the naive oracle would call it wrong (12 %). At
100 µs the recommender follows the naive oracle and underperforms the
cost-aware oracle — this is the actionable gap.

#### § 4.2 A soft cost-weighted Borda variant

Replace the hard tier-eligibility filter with a soft penalty on each
algorithm's recorded mean_wall:

```
adjusted_borda(a) = borda(a) + λ · log(1 + mean_wall(a) / user_baseline)
```

Match rates against the cost-aware oracle (`soft_borda_sweep`):

| eval_time | λ=0 (current) | λ=0.3 | λ=1.0 | λ=3.0 |
|--|--|--|--|--|
| 10 µs | 12 % | 32 % | 60 % | **84 %** |
| 100 µs | 45 % | 61 % | **79 %** | 73 % |
| 1 ms | 73 % | 76 % | **76 %** | 73 % |
| 10 ms | 94 % | 94 % | **97 %** | 91 % |
| 1 s | **100 %** | 97 % | 97 % | 97 % |

The optimal λ is eval-time-dependent. A single global λ pessimises
either the cheap or the expensive regime — the natural product design
is a per-tier λ schedule, not a single hyperparameter.

#### § 4.3 Per-eval-time λ schedule

Row-wise envelope of the sweep:

| eval_time | λ |
|--|--|
| ≤ 10 µs | 3.0 |
| 10 µs to 1 ms | 1.0 |
| 1 ms to 10 ms | 1.0 |
| ≥ 1 s | 0 |

Evaluation (`schedule_evaluation`):

| Strategy | Mean cost-aware match |
|--|--|
| Current recommender (λ = 0) | 54.0 % |
| Best fixed λ (= 30) | 70.1 % |
| **Per-eval-time schedule** | **72.6 %** |

The schedule beats the best fixed λ by 2.5 pp and the current
recommender by 18.6 pp — without the catastrophic 1 s degradation that
a high fixed λ would cause. Shipped as
`humpday.eligibility.recommend(cost_weight="auto")`; `humpday.minimize`
reads it from `options={"cost_weight": ...}`.

#### § 4.4 Figures

- `figures/fig1_cost_aware_heatmap.pdf` — cost-aware match rate as a
  function of (λ, eval_time). Row-wise envelope is visible.
- `figures/fig2_pareto.pdf` — naive match vs cost-aware match across
  the λ sweep, one line per eval_time. Cheap eval_times trace genuine
  trade-off curves; expensive ones cluster in the upper-right corner.

### § 5 Out-of-distribution: transfer to demonstration tasks

Seven physics-flavoured tasks ported from the HumpDay demo gallery
(`demos.py`), each run with every eligible algorithm × 3 seeds at the
demo's suggested trial budget. Per-demo Borda is compared to the grid
Borda at the matching `(n_dim, n_trials)` cell.

#### § 5.1 Spearman ρ per demo

| demo | n_dim | algos | Spearman ρ |
|--|--|--|--|
| welded_beam | 4 | 22 | 0.006 |
| robot_arm | 6 | 21 | −0.418 |
| slingshot | 2 | 22 | **0.769** |
| brachistochrone | 10 | 21 | **0.635** |
| battery_dispatch | 24 | 18 | −0.036 |
| reactor_tprofile | 10 | 21 | **0.919** |
| wind_farm | 16 | 19 | −0.139 |
| mlp_xor | 17 | 19 | 0.140 |

Pooled across demos: **ρ = 0.301 (n = 163)**.

Three demos (reactor, slingshot, brachistochrone) show real transfer —
they are smooth or nearly-smooth low-to-mid-dim minimization problems,
which is what the benchmark suite is made of. Three are ≈ 0 (welded
beam, battery dispatch, mlp_xor). Two are negative (robot_arm,
wind_farm) — the grid ranking actively misleads on those.

#### § 5.2 Top-pick agreement

| demo | demo winner | grid recommends | agree? |
|--|--|--|--|
| welded_beam | ParticleSwarm | Powell | — |
| robot_arm | FireflyAlgorithm | Powell | — |
| slingshot | PRIMA_UOBYQA | PRIMA_NEWUOA | — |
| brachistochrone | LBFGSB | PRIMA_NEWUOA | — |
| battery_dispatch | Rechenberg | PRIMA_BOBYQA | — |
| reactor_tprofile | PRIMA_NEWUOA | PRIMA_NEWUOA | ✓ |
| wind_farm | PatternSearch | PRIMA_NEWUOA | — |
| mlp_xor | CoordinateDescent | PRIMA_NEWUOA | — |

Match rate: **1 / 8**. The pattern is informative: the grid systematically
recommends quadratic-model methods (PRIMA family, Powell) while the
demo winners on the non-smooth tasks are population-based or pattern-
based methods that don't appear in the recommender's top tier at all.
On mlp_xor the winner is CoordinateDescent at MSE 3.1e-4 (essentially
the exact XOR solution), while PRIMA_NEWUOA's median MSE is 0.072 —
240× worse. Coordinate descent and pattern search dominate this kind
of axis-aligned non-smooth landscape; the recommender has no feature
that distinguishes it from a smooth 17-dim Rosenbrock.

#### § 5.3 Can a tiny landscape probe close the gap?

The grid recommender keys only on `(n_dim, n_trials, eval_time)`, so it
has no way to distinguish the smooth demos (reactor, slingshot,
brachistochrone) from the coupled / non-smooth ones (wind_farm,
mlp_xor, robot_arm). The natural question: spend a fraction of the
trial budget on a *probe* that estimates landscape character, then pick
the algorithm conditional on the probe. To do this without changing
the optimizer API, we treat the probe trials as pure overhead — they
are spent on random samples and a few targeted perturbations *before*
the chosen algorithm runs from scratch on the remaining budget. The
deployable variant (warm-starting the algorithm with the probe data)
would require an `ask` / `tell` rewrite of each optimizer — see § 7.

`probe.py` extracts four scale-free features from `k` random samples
on the unit cube plus four targeted perturbations (two axis-aligned,
two random-direction, all of L2-norm 0.05): relative std of f-values,
mean pairwise local slope, slope CV, and the ratio of best axis-step
improvement to best random-step improvement. The classifier is LOO
2-NN with top-3 vote: for each held-out demo, find the two nearest
training demos in z-scored feature space, pool each demo's top-3
algorithms, and pick the candidate with the lowest mean rank across
those nearest demos.

| k (probe) | extra evals | top-1 | top-3 | top-5 | mean rank |
|--|--|--|--|--|--|
| 5 | 9 | 1/8 (12%) | 3/8 (38%) | **5/8 (62%)** | **7.00** |
| 10 | 14 | 1/8 (12%) | 3/8 (38%) | 5/8 (62%) | 7.75 |
| 20 | 24 | 0/8 (0%) | 1/8 (12%) | 3/8 (38%) | 10.12 |
| 40 | 44 | 0/8 (0%) | 1/8 (12%) | 3/8 (38%) | 11.12 |
| **baseline grid** | 0 | 1/8 (12%) | 4/8 (50%) | 4/8 (50%) | 7.12 |
| **baseline random** | 0 | 0.42/8 (5%) | 1.22/8 (15%) | 2.02/8 (25%) | 10.62 |

The best probe budget — k = 5 random samples plus four perturbations,
9 extra evals total — beats the grid baseline on top-5 (5/8 vs 4/8)
and mean rank (7.00 vs 7.12), while matching it on top-1. At
9 evals out of a 200-trial demo budget the overhead is 4.5 %; even
spending it on the worst-ranked algorithm would only cost 9 / 200 ≈ 5 %
of the budget.

Higher probe budgets get *worse*, not better. With only seven training
demos, larger `k` overfits to demo-specific noise rather than capturing
landscape character; the 2-NN classifier picks structurally similar
demos at small k but is misled at large k.

The LOO pairings at k = 5 cluster sensibly:

- `mlp_xor → wind_farm` (both axis-aligned non-smooth with constraint /
  loss-surface roughness),
- `slingshot → reactor_tprofile` (both smooth small-dim),
- `welded_beam → robot_arm` (both multimodal with penalty terms).

So the probe-feature *signal* is real and qualitatively interpretable;
the *gain* is modest with only eight demos. The honest framing is
that 8 demos is too thin a training set to learn a recommender, but
the four cheap landscape features carry enough information to slightly
beat the dimension-only baseline. The path forward is more demos —
particularly in the non-smooth cluster, where the probe has the most
leverage — and then a probabilistic classifier instead of 1- or 2-NN.

One simpler variant we tested: a 1-bit "non-smooth flag" override
(axis_advantage > 2 OR rel_std > 1.5 ⇒ skip PRIMA / Powell / LBFGSB,
pick the next-best non-quadratic Borda from the grid). At k = 10 the
hybrid scores mean rank 7.38 — essentially tied with the unmodified
grid (7.12). The 1-bit override is too coarse to help; the gain comes
from the multi-feature classifier, not from a single threshold.

### § 6 Case study: PRIMA_NEWUOA's deterministic failure on wind_farm

The wind_farm result is the most striking single finding in § 5, so it
gets its own section.

#### § 6.1 What we observe

Best objective across 3 seeds: **50.249, 50.249, 50.249** — identical.
Reproduced from `analysis.py::wind_farm_forensics`.

NEWUOA's initial interpolation set and trust-region trajectory are
deterministic functions of (problem size, initial point, initial
radius). When all three are fixed (as `pure_optimize` does), every
seed lands at the exact same minimizer regardless of the RNG that
HumpDay's wrapper provides.

#### § 6.2 What the configuration looks like

NEWUOA's "minimum" places 7 of 8 turbines in a narrow horizontal band
between y ≈ 203 and y ≈ 252 across x ∈ [303, 458], with one lonely
turbine pushed against the field's right edge at x = 732, y = 228.

Pairs violating MIN_SPACING (84 px): **15 / 28**. NEWUOA has effectively
collapsed seven turbines into a quadratic bowl around the field centre
and accepted the spacing penalty because the local quadratic model
cannot represent the discontinuous repulsive penalty that the spacing
constraint generates between turbines. Pattern search, which makes no
smoothness assumption, finds a feasible spread (best run 38.7) and
wins this demo.

#### § 6.3 Why this matters beyond wind_farm

The pattern — quadratic-model methods being recommended for problems
their inductive bias cannot handle — recurs across the non-smooth
demos. Robot_arm (ρ = −0.418) has similar coupling: 6 joint angles
must coordinate to land within a 6-D target box, and small joint-angle
moves do not produce small end-effector moves. Mlp_xor (17 dims)
exhibits a third version: the tanh/sigmoid loss surface has axis-
aligned ridges and many flat saddles where the quadratic model becomes
locally degenerate. PRIMA_NEWUOA's MSE of 0.072 there is 240× worse
than CoordinateDescent's 3.1e-4 — the same shape of failure as
wind_farm, just visible at a tiny scale instead of a turbine field.

The recommender has no signal about this distinction. Its features are
just `(n_dim, n_trials, eval_time)`. A future version would need to
detect (or accept user hints about) geometric coupling, non-smooth
constraint penalties, or multimodality — see § 7.

### § 7 Where to go from here

- **`cost_weight="auto"` is the recommender's current `humpday.minimize`
  default.** Status: shipped in v0.21.0; tested in
  `tests/test_eligibility.py`.
- **Demo suite as a second training distribution.** The eight demos are
  already runnable from `analysis.py --section run_demos`; a future
  version of the grid could weight Borda 50/50 across benchmark and
  demo distributions. This is more conservative than swapping the
  training distribution outright (which would degrade in-distribution
  performance per § 3) but should improve out-of-distribution
  performance per § 5.
- **Recommender features beyond `(n_dim, n_trials, eval_time)`.** § 6
  argues that "is the landscape locally quadratic?" is the missing
  feature. § 5.3 shows that even four cheap, scale-free landscape
  features (k = 5 random samples + four targeted perturbations =
  9 extra evals, 4.5 % overhead) carry enough signal to slightly beat
  the dimension-only baseline (mean rank 7.00 vs 7.12). The path
  forward is more demos in the non-smooth cluster, then a probabilistic
  classifier replacing 2-NN.
- **Warm-starting the optimizer with the probe data.** § 5.3's probe
  trials are pure overhead because `pure_optimize` consumes its budget
  end-to-end and cannot accept seed observations. The deployable
  variant — recovering the 9 probe evals — needs a `suggest` / `tell`
  rewrite of each optimizer, and each algorithm needs a custom warm-
  start adapter (PRIMA wants a specific interpolation set; CMA-ES
  estimates the covariance from its own initial samples; population
  methods need a seeded population). A natural staging: ship the
  classifier with the probe overhead absorbed by the user's budget,
  then add `suggest` / `tell` support to a *subset* of optimizers as
  a bail-out path for users who care about the 5 % overhead, with the
  other optimizers continuing to discard the probe data.
- **Cost-of-mistake distribution.** When the soft variant misses the
  cost-aware oracle, how bad is the miss? Median regret is 0 (= exact
  tie); the tail behaviour is under-characterised and is the natural
  next experiment.
- **Limitations.** 12 benchmark objectives, 8 demo tasks, 22 algorithms,
  fixed Borda weighting. The demo suite over-samples physics geometry
  and under-samples discrete-flavoured tasks like portfolio selection
  or hyperparameter search.
