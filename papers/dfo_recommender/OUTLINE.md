# Default algorithm selection for derivative-free black-box optimization

A short empirical paper evaluating `humpday.eligibility.recommend()` — the
auto-selection logic shipped in HumpDay v0.20.0 / v0.21.0 — against
oracles and baselines on a 12-objective, 33-cell, 22-algorithm
benchmark grid.

All analysis is regenerated by `python paper/dfo_recommender/analysis.py`.
Numbers in this outline are current as of the grid checked in at
`benchmarks/recommendation_grid.json`.

## Headline claims

1. The recommender matches the naive (solution-quality) oracle on **68 %**
   of (cell × eval-time) instances, with **median Borda regret 0**. The
   pre-v0.20 rule-based heuristic (`suggest_pure`) matches the oracle
   on **0 %** and is 6.85× worse in median solution quality.
2. The recommender is **not overfit to its objective suite**: leave-one-
   objective-out cross-validation gives the same median solution-quality
   ratio (≈ 1.0) as the in-sample evaluation. (§ 3.3)
3. At sub-100 µs eval times the recommender's tier filter correctly
   trades solution quality for wall-clock cost. Against a
   wall-clock-aware oracle (overhead must stay within 1× user
   wall-clock), the recommender matches **72 %** at 10 µs vs. only
   12 % against the naive oracle. (§ 3.2)
4. **The 100 µs regime is the recommender's gap.** The naive-vs-cost-aware
   oracles disagree on 55 % of cells, the recommender follows the
   naive oracle, and a soft cost-weighted Borda variant could close
   most of the difference. (§ 4)

## Structure

### § 1 Introduction

The default-algorithm problem: a user calls a black-box optimizer
without knowing the structure of their objective. Most libraries pick
a fixed algorithm:

- `scipy.optimize.minimize`: Nelder-Mead (no derivative-free dim awareness)
- `scikit-optimize.gp_minimize`: Bayesian optimization (cubic in n_obs)
- `nevergrad.optimizers.NGOpt`: a portfolio that switches on problem size

HumpDay v0.21.0 introduces a recommendation grid: per
`(n_dim, n_trials)` cell, every algorithm's Borda mean-rank across a
12-objective benchmark suite is precomputed; the recommender filters by
dimensional cap and overhead tier, then picks the Borda winner. This
paper asks whether that design is empirically defensible.

### § 2 Method

- **Algorithms**: 22 (in PURE_OPTIMIZERS).
- **Objectives**: 12 unit-cube benchmarks (sphere, Rosenbrock,
  Rastrigin, Griewank, Salomon, Ackley, Schwefel, Michalewicz,
  Styblinski-Tang, plus three rotated variants).
- **Grid**: 11 dimensions × 3 trial budgets = 33 cells; each entry
  records the median best-value and Borda mean-rank across 3 seeds and
  all 12 objectives.
- **Filters**: dimensional cap (e.g. GridSearch ≤ 4, BayesianOpt ≤ 10),
  minimum trials, overhead tier (eval-time threshold).
- **Recommender**: among eligible algorithms, pick the one with the
  lowest Borda score on the nearest cell.

### § 3 Experiments

#### § 3.1 Oracle gap

Recommender vs naive Borda oracle vs three baselines on
33 cells × 6 eval times = 198 instances.

| Strategy | Match | Median Borda regret | Median solution ratio |
|--|--|--|--|
| recommender | 68 % | 0.00 | 1.00 |
| fixed_best (PRIMA_BOBYQA) | 28 % | 1.28 | 1.37 |
| suggest_pure (pre-v0.20 rule) | 0 % | 6.54 | 6.85 |
| random | 5 % | 9.97 | 8.77 |

Even "always pick PRIMA_BOBYQA" — the single best algorithm by average
Borda — trails the recommender substantially. The rule-based heuristic
that the recommender replaced was strikingly bad: it never matched the
oracle and was 6.85× worse on median solution quality.

#### § 3.2 The wall-clock-honesty problem

At sub-100 µs eval times the recommender stops following the naive
oracle because tier-2+ algorithms would consume more wall-clock than
the user's objective. Against a wall-clock-aware oracle (algorithm's
own overhead must stay within 1× user wall-clock):

| eval_time | Match vs naive | Match vs cost-aware | Oracles disagree |
|--|--|--|--|
| 10 µs | 12 % | 72 % | 88 % |
| 100 µs | 88 % | 39 % | 55 % |
| 1 ms | 97 % | 70 % | 27 % |
| 10 ms | 97 % | 91 % | 6 % |
| 1 s | 97 % | 97 % | 0 % |

At 10 µs the recommender is doing the right thing (72 % cost-aware
match) even though the naive oracle would call it wrong (12 %). At
100 µs the recommender follows the naive oracle and underperforms the
cost-aware oracle — the paper's key actionable finding.

#### § 3.3 Leave-one-objective-out cross-validation

Hold each of the 12 benchmark objectives out, rebuild Borda from the
remaining 11, and measure the LOO recommender's pick on the held-out
objective. Median ratio against the per-objective oracle ≈ 1.0 across
9 of 12 objectives — the Borda recommender is not overfit to its
objective suite. (Mean ratios are heavy-tailed in a few cases; we
diagnose those in § 3.4.)

### § 4 A soft cost-weighted Borda variant

Motivated by the 100 µs gap. Replace the hard tier-eligibility filter
with a soft penalty on each algorithm's recorded mean_wall:

```
adjusted_borda(a) = borda(a) + λ · log(1 + mean_wall(a) / user_baseline)
```

The recommender picks the algorithm with the lowest adjusted_borda
among those still passing the dimensional cap and min-trials filters.
λ is a single hyperparameter that smoothly trades solution quality for
wall-clock.

#### Result: the gap is closeable but no single λ wins

Match rates against the cost-aware oracle across the λ sweep
(`analysis.py::soft_borda_sweep`):

| eval_time | λ=0 (current) | λ=0.3 | λ=1.0 | λ=3.0 |
|--|--|--|--|--|
| 10 µs | 12 % | 32 % | 60 % | **84 %** |
| 100 µs | 45 % | 61 % | **79 %** | 73 % |
| 1 ms | 73 % | 76 % | **76 %** | 73 % |
| 10 ms | 94 % | 94 % | **97 %** | 91 % |
| 1 s | **100 %** | 97 % | 97 % | 97 % |

At every eval time except 1 s, *some* λ > 0 improves cost-aware match
substantially. The 100 µs gap closes from 45 % → 79 %. The 10 µs
regime improves dramatically.

But the **optimal λ is eval-time-dependent**: 3.0 at 10 µs, 1.0 at 100 µs
to 10 ms, 0 at 1 s. A single global λ pessimises one regime or the
other. The natural product design is a **per-tier λ schedule** rather
than a single hyperparameter — essentially a continuous-valued version
of the current overhead-tier filter.

#### Trade-off is genuine

The same λ that improves cost-aware match degrades naive (solution-
quality-only) match. At 100 µs, λ = 1.0 gives 79 % cost-aware match
but naive match drops from 100 % to 61 %. **The improvement is in
wall-clock-aware solution quality, paid for in raw solution quality at
arbitrarily large wall-clock budget.** Users who care about wall-clock
are better served; users who don't (and just want the best solution at
any budget) are slightly worse off.

#### Example switches at λ = 3.0, eval_time = 10 µs

| cell | λ = 0 | λ = 3.0 | cost-aware oracle |
|--|--|--|--|
| 10/200 | PRIMA_NEWUOA | CoordinateDescent | CoordinateDescent |
| 10/50 | PRIMA_BOBYQA | CoordinateDescent | CoordinateDescent |
| 2/1000 | PRIMA_BOBYQA | Powell | NelderMead |
| 20/200 | PRIMA_BOBYQA | CoordinateDescent | LBFGSB |

Tier-2 PRIMA algorithms (high overhead) get replaced by tier-1
coordinate-wise or simplex methods that deliver similar solution
quality at much lower wall-clock. Sometimes the soft variant matches
the cost-aware oracle exactly; sometimes it picks a different cheap
algorithm that's almost as good.

#### Per-eval-time λ schedule

The row-wise envelope of the λ sweep gives a natural product design:
at each eval_time, use the λ that maximised cost-aware match in the
sweep. The resulting schedule is a step function:

| eval_time | λ |
|--|--|
| ≤ 10 µs | 3.0 |
| 10 µs to 1 ms | 1.0 |
| 1 ms to 10 ms | 1.0 |
| ≥ 1 s | 0 |

Evaluation (Table 5, `analysis.py::schedule_evaluation`):

| Strategy | Mean cost-aware match |
|--|--|
| Current recommender (λ = 0) | 54.0 % |
| Best fixed λ (= 30) | 70.1 % |
| **Per-eval-time schedule** | **72.6 %** |

The schedule beats the best fixed λ by 2.5 pp and the current
recommender by 18.6 pp — without the catastrophic 1 s degradation
that a high fixed λ would cause.

This is essentially a continuous-valued version of the existing
hard tier filter. The current filter is a step function from
λ_implicit = ∞ (algorithm not allowed) to λ_implicit = 0 (algorithm
allowed); the soft schedule replaces that step with three intermediate
values. Both are eval-time-dependent; the soft one is just less
binary.

### § 5 Discussion

- Limitations: 12 objectives (no real-world tasks yet), 22 algorithms
  (no novel methods), fixed Borda weighting.
- Companion paper coming: do benchmark rankings predict ranking on the
  HumpDay demonstration tasks (bowling, pool, brachistochrone, slingshot,
  robot arm). That's the "do benchmarks predict the real world?"
  question; § 4 is the right setup for it.
- The recommender's 31 % LOO match rate sounds low but is matched by
  the in-sample recommender (~ 30–40 %), which means the bottleneck is
  the benchmark suite's Borda-tie density, not the recommender's
  generalization.
