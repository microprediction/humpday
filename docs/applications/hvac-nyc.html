🏢 NYC Building HVAC Dispatch

A commercial floor in Manhattan has four ways to keep itself comfortable: an electric heat pump (efficient when it's warm outside, mediocre below freezing), a gas boiler (constant efficiency, gas commodity price), Con Edison district steam (the world's largest urban steam loop — premium-priced), and an electric chiller. Electricity from NYISO Zone J swings from ~$30/MWh overnight to over $200/MWh at the 5 pm peak. Search for a control policy (linear in zone state) that minimises the daily total energy bill plus comfort penalty, averaged across four real-weather days from a NYC year. 16-D continuous problem.

What's happening

The zone is a single-RC thermal model — capacitance C = 10 kBtu/°F, overall envelope conductance UA = 0.8 kBtu/h/°F, so the natural time constant τ ≈ 12.5 h. Stepped hourly, with setpoint 72 °F and a comfort band of 68–76 °F enforced during occupied hours (8 am – 6 pm). The outside temperature follows a smooth diurnal curve with a daily peak around 2 pm.

Four dispatchable sources, with cost structures that pull in different directions:

  • Heat pump — COP scales with outdoor temperature (≈3.5 at 60 °F, ≈1.5 at 0 °F). Pays the volatile NYISO LMP.
  • Gas boiler — flat 92 % efficiency, paid in $/MMBtu of natural gas (nearly constant intraday).
  • Con Ed steam — direct heat, no on-site combustion. Highest unit cost, with a morning ramp.
  • Electric chiller — COP drops as outside gets hotter. Also pays the LMP.

Each scenario simulates a 24-hour day from a different point of a NYC year — a January cold snap, a mild winter day, a mild summer day, and a July heat wave. The score is the mean of total daily cost (energy + comfort penalty at $1.5/°F-h during occupancy) across all four days.

The 16-D decision variable is the parameters of a linear control policy: four sources, each driven by a sigmoid of (bias + zone error + outdoor demand + electricity-price excursion). The optimum trades off when to use each source against the current state — a "good thermostat" gets to ~$70/day, but the optimum gets close to ~$30/day by riding the heat pump when it's warm and the LMP is cheap, switching to the boiler during the 4–7 pm peak, and barely touching steam.

Single-zone RC simplification of an EnergyPlus-style building model. Real BMS optimization adds latent loads, weather forecasting, occupancy stochasticity, multi-zone coupling, and demand-response tariff signals — but the core structure (find a policy that minimises an expected cost over a distribution of days) is the same.
