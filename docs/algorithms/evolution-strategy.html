Evolution Strategy (μ+λ)

Classical Evolutionary Optimization

← Back to Optimizer Competition
Evolution Strategy is one of the oldest evolutionary algorithms, dating to Rechenberg and Schwefel's work in 1960s Berlin. The (μ+λ) variant maintains μ parents, generates λ mutated offspring each generation, and selects the best μ individuals from the combined parent+offspring pool to form the next generation. It is the conceptual ancestor of CMA-ES.

Performance Characteristics

  • Best for: Continuous unconstrained optimization on moderate-dimensional problems
  • Dimensions: Works well from 2 up to a few dozen; favoured for "high-dimensional complex" routing in HumpDay's adaptive optimizer
  • Function evaluations: Each generation costs up to λ evaluations; total budget is set by n_trials
  • Convergence: Reliable progress on smooth landscapes; can stall on highly multimodal surfaces without self-adaptation
  • Robustness: Tolerates noise via its population-and-selection structure; less robust than CMA-ES because step size σ is fixed

Algorithm Overview

  1. Initialize μ parents uniformly at random in the unit cube.
  2. Mutate: pick a parent at random, perturb by σ·𝒩(0,I), clip to the unit cube. Repeat λ times.
  3. Select: sort the combined (parent + offspring) pool by fitness and keep the best μ.
  4. Repeat until the evaluation budget is exhausted.

Notation: the "+" in (μ+λ) indicates elitism — parents compete against their own offspring. The alternative (μ,λ) variant discards parents each generation; HumpDay implements the elitist (μ+λ) form.
